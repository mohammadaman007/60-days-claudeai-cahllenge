Here are the core takeaways this simulation is built to teach:

**1. Speed vs. accuracy is a real trade-off**
Every alert has a countdown. Waiting for perfect information (or hitting "Delay") often costs more than acting fast on a good-enough read — but acting fast on the *wrong* lever costs even more. The game rewards fast + relevant, not just fast.

**2. Match the response to the root cause**
Each disruption has one action that's genuinely aligned with it (e.g. reroute trucks for a weather shutdown, backup supplier for a machine failure) and others that look proactive but don't address the actual bottleneck (e.g. increasing production doesn't help a customs hold). The lesson: busy activity isn't the same as solving the right problem.

**3. Ignoring risk doesn't make it disappear**
"Ignore" always costs more than any active response — small problems left unaddressed compound into service and revenue damage.

**4. Deferred decisions carry deferred risk, not zero risk**
"Delay Decision" feels safe in the moment, but the consequence just arrives later — sometimes worse, because the situation kept deteriorating while unaddressed.

**5. Everything is a trade-off across KPIs, not a single score**
A "good" fix (like air freight) often improves one metric (service level) while hurting another (operating cost). Real ops leadership is optimizing a *portfolio* of metrics, not maximizing one in isolation.

**6. Pressure compounds under load**
As more alerts stack up simultaneously and arrive faster, the same decision quality gets harder to sustain — mirroring how real supply chains degrade under multi-front disruption rather than one clean issue at a time.
