Concept · 01

Headlines are written to be clicked, not just to be true.

A headline's job is to get your attention in under two seconds. That pressure pushes writers toward stronger, punchier, more dramatic words than the actual story supports. This gap between the claim and the reality is called a headline–content mismatch, and it's one of the most common tricks in your feed — used by clickbait sites and legitimate outlets alike.

Why it matters

Most people only read the headline. If it overstates the story, that overstatement becomes what you "know" — even if you never open the article.

In everyday life

Look for absolute words (proves, cures, bans, destroys), manufactured secrecy ("they don't want you to know"), and false urgency ("starting next month").

    Concept · 02

    Strong feelings make you stop scrolling — and stop thinking.

    Fear, anger, and urgency are the three most reliable ways to get someone to share something without checking it first. Posts engineered around emotion often use vague accusations, artificial scarcity ("only 100 spots"), or a sense of secrecy being revealed. The feeling arrives before the facts do — and by then, you've already hit share.

    Why it matters

    Emotional posts spread faster than neutral ones. The stronger the feeling, the less likely people are to verify before reacting.

    In everyday life

    Notice when a post asks for an emotional reaction (rage, fear, envy) before it gives you any specific, checkable fact.

      Three habits to build

      • 01

        Pause before you share. If a post makes you feel something strongly within the first line, that's a signal to slow down, not speed up.

        Read past the headline. Open the article and check whether the body actually supports the claim the headline made.
