The roast

What a recruiter actually thinks in the first 3 seconds — no sugarcoating.

Headline

4/10
"Mohammad Aman | Student at Maharana Pratap Group of Institutions, 3rd year | Data Analyst | Python | numpy | Pandas | Data Visualization | Advance with AI Excel | PowerBI | Tableau | MySQL | AI Tools |."

❌ The problem: This is a keyword dump, not a headline. Eleven items separated by pipes with no verb, no value proposition, and a stray period at the end. It reads like a tag cloud, not a person.

🧠 Why it hurts: Recruiters skim headlines in under 2 seconds. A wall of tool names tells them "this person knows some software" — it doesn't tell them what you can actually do with it, or why they should care.

🔍 The invisible cost: You DO rank on some keyword searches because the tools are all there — that part is working. But you lose the click. Recruiters open 3-5 profiles per search; yours won't be one of the ones they open, because nothing differentiates you from the other 40 students listing the same 8 tools.

About — first 2 lines

0/10

❌ The problem: There is no About section. This is the single biggest gap on your profile.

🧠 Why it hurts: The first two lines of About are the most valuable real estate on LinkedIn — they show before the "see more" click and often show directly in search results. An empty About section means zero pitch, zero personality, zero reason to keep reading.

🔍 The invisible cost: Recruiters searching "data analyst fresher Python" land on your profile, see a busy headline and nothing else, and move on. You're filtered out before anyone reads your actual skills.

About — full section

0/10

❌ The problem: Same as above — nothing written yet.

🧠 Why it hurts: This is where you're supposed to prove you can turn tools into outcomes. Without it, "Python, Power BI, Tableau" is just a list a bot could generate.

🔍 The invisible cost: You're currently indistinguishable from thousands of other 3rd-year students who took the same online courses. The About section is what makes you memorable — right now nothing does.

Experience

2/10

❌ The problem: No Experience entry exists. You do have a real e-commerce sales analysis project, but it isn't on your profile at all, and even in our conversation it was described as four generic verbs: "cleaned data, built a dashboard, found insights, made a report."

🧠 Why it hurts: Recruiters look at Experience (or Projects) to answer one question: "can this person actually do the job, or do they just know the vocabulary?" Right now there's no evidence either way.

🔍 The invisible cost: You already did the hard part — the project. You're just not getting credit for it. That's the most fixable problem on this whole profile.

Skills & keywords

6/10

❌ The problem: The right keywords exist (Python, Pandas, NumPy, Power BI, Tableau, MySQL, Excel) but they're crammed into the headline instead of properly structured in the Skills section, and vague entries like "AI Tools" and "Advance with AI Excel" dilute the list.

🧠 Why it hurts: LinkedIn's search algorithm weighs the dedicated Skills section, endorsements, and how skills are reinforced across About/Experience — not just the headline. A headline-only keyword strategy is fragile.

🔍 The invisible cost: You're search-visible for the wrong reason (headline stuffing) and under-visible for the right one (a real, endorsed, reinforced Skills section).

The rebuild

Every rewrite comes with the strategy behind it — copy it, but understand it first.

Headline — 3 options

Option 1 · keyword-optimized (for search visibility)
Data Analyst | Python, Pandas, Power BI, Tableau, SQL | Turning Raw Data into Clear Business Decisions
Use this if your main goal is showing up in recruiter searches for "data analyst," "Python," "Power BI." Front-loads the exact terms recruiters filter by, then closes with a value line instead of trailing off.
Option 2 · value-proposition (for internship applications)
Aspiring Data Analyst — I turn messy spreadsheets into dashboards people actually use | Python · Power BI · Tableau
Use this on applications and when messaging recruiters directly. It answers "what do you do for me" in the first clause, which is what a hiring manager scanning 200 applicants actually wants.
Option 3 · authority style (for posting and networking)
3rd Year CS Student → Data Analyst | Learning in public: Python, SQL, Power BI, Tableau | Sharing real dashboards, not just certificates
Use this once you start posting content (Day 2 of the plan). It sets an expectation — "this person shares real work" — that pulls in recruiters and other students building similar skills.

Since your goal is landing an internship/job, start with Option 2 now, switch to Option 1 once your Skills section is fully built out, and consider Option 3 once you're posting weekly.

About section — full rewrite

Hook (before "see more")
Most dashboards look impressive. Mine are built to answer one question: what should you do next?
Story
I'm a 3rd-year student building toward a career as a Data Analyst. I work across Python, Excel, Power BI, Tableau, and MySQL — not to collect tools, but to turn raw, messy data into decisions someone can actually act on.
Proof
In my e-commerce sales analysis project, I cleaned and structured a full year of sales data, built an interactive dashboard, and found that just 3 products drove roughly 60% of revenue — with weekend sales running about 30% higher than weekdays.
CTA
Open to data analyst internships and entry-level roles. If you're hiring or just want to talk data, message me — or check the Featured section below for the project breakdown.
Data AnalystPythonPandasNumPyPower BITableauMySQLExcelData VisualizationData Cleaning

Why this structure: the hook is a question, which is proven to increase "see more" clicks over a statement. The story stays in plain language on purpose — corporate phrasing ("leveraged synergies") reads as inauthentic from a student. The proof section is the only place with numbers, because that's what a recruiter's eye jumps to. Keywords are woven into sentences, not listed, so they read naturally to a human while still indexing for search.

I buried my best finding (3 products = ~60% of revenue) on the last slide instead of the first. None of these are Python or Power BI problems. They're thinking problems. Still learning.
