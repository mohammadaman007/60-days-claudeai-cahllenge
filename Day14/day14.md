Based on the red flag analysis, here are the key things you can take away as a job seeker:

**How to read a job description critically**

The JD text itself isn't always where the danger hides. This role had clean, clear language — but the company information revealed the real story. Always research the company separately, not just the role.

**The salary silence rule**

When salary is missing, it almost never benefits you. Companies that are proud of their compensation advertise it. Withholding it is a negotiation tactic that starts before you even apply.

**The turnover triangle**

Three signals together — high turnover + unrealistic targets + many open positions — form a pattern that's very hard to explain away innocently. One of those alone? Maybe fine. All three? That's a systemic problem, not a coincidence.

**Skill stacking is a budget signal**

When a single role wants SQL, Python, BI tools, ETL, ML, and statistics all at once, it usually means they want one person to do the work of two or three. That's not a challenge — it's an underfunded team.

**The JD tone can mask company reality**

No hustle-culture language in the JD doesn't mean the culture is healthy. The writing style of a job post is often crafted by an HR team or recruiter — the real culture shows up in employee reviews and turnover data.

**Interviews work both ways**

The five questions showed you that interviews aren't just for the employer to evaluate you — they're your only real chance to pressure-test what you can't verify from the outside. Asking direct questions about tenure, targets, and salary is professional, not aggressive.

**Overall lesson:** A job description tells you what they want. Your job is to find out what it's actually like to work there — and those are two very different things.
